How Stars Shine

Stars shine because of nuclear fusion happening deep in their cores. Below is how it works:-

Gravity pulls hydrogen atoms together.

Gravity causes vast clouds of hydrogen gas in space to slowly collapse inward. As the atoms move closer together, the cloud becomes denser and warmer, forming the early structure of a star.

They fuse and form helium.

As the core becomes hotter and denser, hydrogen atoms begin to collide and fuse together. This nuclear fusion process converts hydrogen into helium at the star’s center.

This fusion releases tremendous energy.

The fusion of hydrogen into helium releases enormous amounts of energy. This energy creates outward pressure that balances gravity and stabilizes the star.

That energy becomes light and heat.

The energy produced in the core travels outward and escapes into space as light and heat. This radiation is what makes the star shine and provides warmth to its surroundings.

ASTRONOMY DISCOVERY

Solar System Rocky Planets

Explore the terrestrial planets that share our cosmic neighborhood in the inner solar system.

SUN

The Sun is a massive star at the center of our solar system, made mostly of hydrogen and helium. It produces energy through nuclear fusion, where hydrogen atoms combine to form helium, releasing enormous amounts of light and heat. This energy drives Earth’s climate and makes life possible. The Sun’s strong gravity holds all the planets, moons, asteroids, and comets in orbit around it

Mercury

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun and the smallest in the solar system. It has a rocky surface covered in craters and almost no atmosphere, causing temperatures to swing from extremely hot during the day to freezing cold at night.

Venus

Venus is similar in size to Earth but has a thick atmosphere made mostly of carbon dioxide with clouds of sulfuric acid. This creates a powerful greenhouse effect, making Venus the hottest planet. It also rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction of most planets.

Earth

Earth is a rocky planet with liquid water on its surface and an atmosphere rich in nitrogen and oxygen. It supports a wide variety of life and has a magnetic field that protects it from harmful solar radiation..

Mars

Mars is a cold, rocky planet known for its red color, caused by iron oxide in its soil. It has the largest volcano and canyon in the solar system and shows evidence that liquid water once flowed on its surface.

Jupiter

Jupiter is the largest planet and a gas giant made mostly of hydrogen and helium. It has a strong magnetic field, dozens of moons, and the Great Red Spot, a massive storm that has lasted for centuries..

Saturn

Saturn is a gas giant best known for its extensive ring system made of ice and rock. It has many moons, including Titan, which has lakes of liquid methane and ethane.

Uranus

Uranus is an ice giant with a blue-green color caused by methane in its atmosphere. It is tilted on its side, leading to extreme seasonal changes, and has a cold, icy interior.

Neptune

Neptune is an ice giant and the most distant planet from the Sun. It has deep blue coloring, powerful winds, and massive storms, making it one of the most dynamic planets in the solar system.

FEATURED RESEARCH

Comet Trajectory

In astronomy, a comet's trajectory serves as a bridge between the chaotic birth of the solar system and modern celestial mechanics, acting as a "fossilized" record of the gravitational forces that shaped our neighborhood. These highly eccentric paths, governed by Kepler’s Laws and Newton’s gravity, trace the history of planetary migration, as most comets were flung into the Oort Cloud or Kuiper Belt by the gravity of giant planets billions of years ago. By studying these orbits, astronomers can detect "non-gravitational" forces caused by outgassing, which revealed the existence of the solar wind, and monitor potential Earth-crossers for planetary defense. Ultimately, the trajectory of a comet is a roadmap that leads scientists back to the primordial ices and organic molecules that may have seeded life on Earth, proving that these icy wanderers are fundamental to our understanding of both our origins and our cosmic safety.
